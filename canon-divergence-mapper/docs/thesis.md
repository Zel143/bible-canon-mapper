# Mapping Canon Divergence: A Computational Comparison of Protestant, Catholic, and Orthodox Biblical Traditions

**Status: draft, revised after a scope change (2026-07-22).** This synthesizes the completed pipeline (`src/`, `notebooks/01`–`04`) and the research log in `docs/historical_notes.md` into thesis form. It is not a finished submission — see the "Limitations and Open Items" section (5.4) for what still needs work before this goes to a committee.

**Scope change, 2026-07-22:** the project's text pipeline is now restricted to the Protestant Bible (66-book canon, King James Version) as its sole source text, by deliberate choice — see `context/STATUS.md`. Douay-Rheims and Septuagint text were fetched and used in an earlier version of this project (recoverable at commit `f5b4236` and earlier) to compare protocanonical against deuterocanonical *content* by topic modeling; that comparison is no longer possible within a Protestant-only text corpus, since the deuterocanonical books fall outside it entirely. Two of the three original sub-questions are unaffected by this change — canon overlap (Sub-Question 1) and translation lineage (Sub-Question 3) are built from canon-list *metadata* and historical/bibliographic claims, not from Bible text, so both still cover all three traditions as before. Sub-Question 2 (deuterocanonical vs. protocanonical topic modeling) is no longer answerable in this scope and has been replaced with an Old Testament vs. New Testament topic comparison *within* the Protestant/KJV corpus (Section 3.5, 4.3). This is a narrower, intra-canon question — it no longer speaks to canon *divergence* the way the original comparison did, and that tradeoff is stated plainly rather than papered over.

## Abstract

Protestant, Catholic, and Orthodox Bibles disagree about which books belong in the Old Testament — 66, 73, and roughly 77–81 books respectively, depending on jurisdiction — and that disagreement is often discussed in exclusively historical or theological terms: council decisions, confessional statements, patristic testimony. This thesis treats the disagreement computationally instead, building a small open-source pipeline that (1) represents canon membership as a structured, queryable dataset rather than prose description, (2) traces which translations and source-language texts fed into which later Bibles, with each claim dated and cited, and (3), within the Protestant/KJV corpus specifically, tests using topic modeling whether Old and New Testament passages are thematically distinguishable. The canon-overlap and translation-lineage results (Sections 4.1–4.2) draw on all three traditions, since both are built from canon-list metadata and historical/bibliographic sourcing rather than from comparative Bible text. The topic-modeling component was originally a cross-canon comparison — deuterocanonical books (Catholic/Orthodox-only) against protocanonical ones (shared by all three) — but the project's text source was deliberately restricted to the Protestant Bible partway through (2026-07-22, see the note above), which puts the deuterocanonical books outside the corpus entirely; that analysis, including a genre-confound stress test and a topic-count/seed sensitivity sweep, is preserved in git history but is no longer part of this thesis's active results. In its place, Section 4.3 reports a topic-distribution comparison between Old and New Testament passages in the same KJV corpus: LDA finds two topics dominated by Christ/Gospel vocabulary that are almost entirely New Testament (a combined ~81% of NT passages against ~6% of OT passages), against a spread of law/cultic and royal-narrative topics concentrated in the Old Testament; the gap (Jensen-Shannon distance 0.765) is far outside a 3,000-shuffle permutation null (p = 0.0003). This is a real but substantially weaker result for the thesis's stated research question than the original cross-canon comparison, since Old vs. New Testament differ in language register, era, and genre simultaneously and no genre-controlled test has yet been run for it — flagged explicitly in Section 5.4 rather than presented as settling anything. The translation lineage graph, unaffected by the scope change, remains built from primary and secondary historical sources and corrects two claims that seemed intuitive but turn out to be unsupported or anachronistic once checked. The result is a small, reusable toolkit and dataset for canon comparison — at the metadata and lineage level, still genuinely cross-canon — plus a worked, honestly-caveated example of applying standard NLP methods to biblical text.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Research Question

How do textual, source, and historical differences across the Protestant, Catholic, and Orthodox biblical canons manifest quantifiably — in content overlap, translation lineage, and thematic emphasis?

**Sub-questions:**

1. Which books are unique to each canon, and how can this be represented and visualized as a set-overlap or graph structure?
2. *(Original formulation, no longer answerable in this project's current scope — see the scope-change note above.)* Does topic modeling reveal a measurable difference in emphasis between the deuterocanonical books (Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch) and the protocanonical books all three traditions share? **Current formulation:** within the Protestant/KJV corpus, does topic modeling reveal a measurable difference in emphasis between Old and New Testament passages?
3. Can a translation lineage — which source texts and prior translations fed into which later ones — be constructed and *sourced*, rather than asserted, to show where each tradition's canon and text ultimately trace back to?

### 1.2 Motivation

The proximate motivation was a specific question: what does the Bible say about the intercession of saints and prayer for the dead, versus what Catholic tradition holds? Chasing that question honestly led to 2 Maccabees 12:38–46, which explicitly commends prayer and sacrifice for the dead — a passage that is canonical Scripture for Catholics and Orthodox and has no scriptural status for Protestants, because 2 Maccabees is a deuterocanonical book: affirmed as canon at the Councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397), reaffirmed dogmatically by the Council of Trent (1546) in response to the Reformation, but moved to a non-canonical "Apocrypha" section in Luther's 1534 Bible [4][5][6]. A doctrinal disagreement that looked, on its face, like a dispute over interpreting a shared text turned out to be partly a downstream consequence of a *prior* disagreement about which texts count as Scripture at all.

That distinction — theological interpretation of shared Scripture versus disagreement rooted in different source texts — is not always obvious from the outside, and it is not always the case. As a contrast case, 2 Kings 13:20–21 (a dead man revived by contact with the bones of the prophet Elisha) involves the same broad pattern as 2 Maccabees — God acting through the remains of a deceased holy person — but is protocanonical, accepted without dispute in all three traditions [7]. The doctrinal split over relics and intercession is not a split over whether that pattern appears in universally accepted Scripture; it does. It is specifically about which later, contested books elaborate that pattern into practice. This pairing — one contested passage, one uncontested passage, on a related theme — became the seed case for the whole project: pick a passage, check its canon status across traditions, then check whether the *content* of contested and uncontested material is thematically distinguishable at scale, not just in this one pair. **Note on scope (2026-07-22):** the topic-modeling test of that exact question — contested (deuterocanonical) vs. uncontested (protocanonical) content — was run and is described in git history, but the project's text source was subsequently narrowed to the Protestant Bible only, which removes the deuterocanonical books from the active corpus. The 2 Maccabees/2 Kings pairing above still motivates the *project*, including the canon-overlap and translation-lineage components (Sections 4.1–4.2), but the topic-modeling component (Section 4.3) is no longer a direct test of it — see the scope-change note preceding the Abstract.

This project also gave a natural place to bring quantitative training to bear on a question usually settled by citing councils and creeds. As a computer science / data science project, it treats the canon-divergence question the way one would treat any other corpus-comparison problem: set overlap, topic modeling, and lineage-graph construction, applied to a domain (biblical canon history) that is not usually approached with these tools.

### 1.3 Scope and Guardrails

This project is **descriptive, not evaluative**: it documents what happened historically and why (Reformation-era debates over the Apocrypha, Jerome's *hebraica veritas* position, conciliar decisions) without adjudicating which canon is theologically correct. This is both a methodological commitment and what makes the project viable as a computer science / data science thesis rather than a theology thesis: the computational contribution (a reusable canon-comparison pipeline, a sourced lineage graph, a topic-modeling result) is the deliverable, and the historical material is treated as data to be modeled, not doctrine to be settled.

## 2. Related Work

Digital humanities projects on biblical corpora (e.g., STEP Bible, Blue Letter Bible's tools) provide the closest prior art for structured, queryable biblical text, though typically oriented toward lookup and cross-referencing rather than comparative set/topic analysis across canon traditions. Computational stylometry and authorship studies on biblical texts (a substantial existing literature, not surveyed in detail here) establish that standard NLP methods are applicable to this text domain. Canon-comparison scholarship proper is extensive but almost entirely historical and non-computational — councils, patristic testimony, confessional documents — and is the primary source material this project draws its lineage-graph and case-study citations from (see References and `docs/historical_notes.md`).

## 3. Data and Methodology

### 3.1 Canon Definitions

Canon membership for each tradition is stored as structured data (`data/metadata/canon_lists.json`), not prose: the Protestant list (39 Old Testament + 27 New Testament books) is the base; the Catholic list adds 7 deuterocanonical books (Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch) plus textual additions to Esther and Daniel not counted as separate books, for 73 total; the Orthodox list adds 4 more (1 Esdras, 3 Maccabees, Prayer of Manasseh, Psalm 151) under one commonly cited Greek/Rahlfs-based baseline, for 77 total. `src/data_loader.load_canon_list(tradition)` loads these programmatically. The Orthodox list is explicitly documented as one baseline among several — Slavonic, Georgian, and Ethiopian jurisdictions include additional books (2 Esdras/4 Ezra, 4 Maccabees, Jubilees, 1 Enoch) not represented here; cross-checking against a specific jurisdiction remains open (Section 5.4).

### 3.2 Source Texts

Three public-domain source translations were fetched and parsed into one plain-text file per book (`src/fetch_kjv.py`, `src/fetch_douay_rheims.py`, `src/fetch_septuagint.py`):

- **King James Version** (Project Gutenberg eBook #10) — Protestant, 66 books, Masoretic-Hebrew/Textus-Receptus-Greek based. As of the 2026-07-22 scope change (see the note preceding the Abstract), this is the only source text actively used by the topic-modeling pipeline (Sections 3.5, 4.3).
- **Douay-Rheims**, Challoner revision (Project Gutenberg eBook #1581) — full 73-book Catholic canon in one consistent, Vulgate-based translation. Fetched and used in an earlier version of this project's topic-modeling comparison (protocanonical vs. deuterocanonical content); no longer part of the active text pipeline, though the fetcher and raw data remain in the repository.
- **Brenton's Septuagint** (eBible.org, USFM format) — the full 50-book Orthodox Old Testament (Brenton's translation has no New Testament) from one consistent Greek-based translation. Same status as Douay-Rheims: fetched, used previously, not part of the active pipeline.

Each fetcher required nontrivial text-processing work beyond a bulk download: matching Gutenberg section headings to canonical book names, handling archaic spelling variants (e.g. "Josue" for Joshua, "Machabees" for Maccabees in Douay-Rheims), splitting Psalm 151 out of a merged Psalms file, reassembling Daniel and Baruch from USFM files that split out their deuterocanonical portions (Susanna, Bel and the Dragon, and the Epistle of Jeremy respectively) as separate books rather than folding them in as the Vulgate tradition does. A real encoding bug was found and fixed during this work: `Path.write_text` on Windows was doubling the already-present `\r\n` line endings in the downloaded Gutenberg text into `\r\r\n`, which silently truncated wrapped verses during later cleaning — caught by actually inspecting output rather than assuming a successful download meant correct output. These fetchers and the bug fix remain relevant even though only the KJV is used downstream now, since the Douay-Rheims and Septuagint text is still available for any future work that reinstates the cross-canon topic comparison (Section 6).

### 3.3 Canonical Source Selection

Prior to 2026-07-22, a shared book (e.g. Genesis) existing in up to three different translations was resolved per-tradition — Protestant→KJV, Catholic→Douay-Rheims, Orthodox→Septuagint (OT) with a KJV fallback for the New Testament, since Brenton's translation has none. That mapping is preserved in git history. As of the scope change, `src/align_corpus.CANONICAL_SOURCES` maps only `protestant → kjv`; requesting text for any other tradition now raises an explicit error rather than silently falling back to a different translation. This removes the need for the translation-consistency argument the earlier version of this section made (comparing protocanonical and deuterocanonical content only makes sense if both come from the same translation) — there is now exactly one tradition and one source text in the active pipeline, so the question does not arise for Section 4.3's OT-vs-NT comparison, which draws every passage from the same KJV translation by construction.

### 3.4 Corpus Cleaning

`src/build_processed_corpus.py` reads each tradition's canonical-source text via `align_corpus.load_book_text` and writes cleaned plain text to `data/processed/<tradition>/<book>.txt`. As of the 2026-07-22 scope change this produces 66 files, all under `data/processed/protestant/` (previously 216: 66 Protestant + 73 Catholic + 77 Orthodox — the Catholic and Orthodox directories were deleted when the pipeline was narrowed, since they are fully regenerable from the still-present raw Douay-Rheims/Septuagint data and fetchers if needed later). The script also writes `data/processed/corpus_manifest.json`, which still records the full three-tradition canon overlap matrix (Section 3.1's metadata, unaffected by the scope change) alongside per-book source/word/character counts for the one tradition with active text. Cleaning removes non-scriptural editorial matter (book introductions, "Chapter N" headings, per-chapter summary blurbs present in the Gutenberg KJV edition) and strips `chapter:verse` markers — including markers embedded mid-paragraph in chapters formatted without per-verse line breaks (e.g. Matthew's genealogy) — down to continuous prose.

### 3.5 Topic Modeling Methodology

**Revised 2026-07-22** to match the scope change: books in the Protestant/KJV corpus are labeled **Old Testament** (39 books) or **New Testament** (27 books) by list position (`data_loader.load_canon_list('protestant')` returns OT books followed by NT books), then split into passages of 150 words each (final partial passage per book dropped if under 30 words), yielding 5,262 passages (4,059 Old Testament, 1,203 New Testament) across all 66 books. The prior version of this section described the same chunking methodology applied to protocanonical/deuterocanonical labels on the 73-book Catholic corpus (5,953 passages); that analysis is preserved in git history. Passages, not whole books or chapters, remain the unit of analysis for the same reason as before: a single topic label per book is too coarse to say anything about emphasis *within* a book, and chapter-level chunking is unavailable because chapter markers are removed during corpus cleaning (Section 3.4).

Passages are vectorized as bag-of-words (`sklearn.feature_extraction.text.CountVectorizer`, English stopwords removed, `max_df=0.9`, `min_df=3`, vocabulary capped at 2,000 terms — 5,262 passages × 2,000 terms) and fit with Latent Dirichlet Allocation [15] (`sklearn.decomposition.LatentDirichletAllocation`, 12 topics, `random_state=42`, 20 iterations). Each passage is assigned its highest-probability (dominant) topic. LDA was kept over an embedding-based method (e.g. BERTopic) for the same practical reason as before (Section 5.4): avoiding a multi-gigabyte dependency install not justified for this scope. `N_TOPICS=12` is carried over unchanged from the prior corpus's tuning result (Section 4.5's historical K-sweep, run on the 73-book Catholic corpus) rather than re-tuned for this different corpus — flagged as an open item in Section 5.4, not a validated choice for OT-vs-NT specifically.

### 3.6 Translation Lineage Methodology

`src/graph_builder.build_translation_lineage_graph()` constructs a directed acyclic graph of 14 texts and 20 lineage edges, each carrying an approximate date and a note — either a citation into the reference list below or a qualifier distinguishing the edge type (e.g. "standardization, not translation" for the Masoretes' codification of an existing Hebrew tradition; "reference, not primary source" for a text a translator consulted without translating from). Every edge was checked individually against historical sources rather than assumed; two edges present in an earlier, explicitly-flagged-as-placeholder version of this graph turned out to be wrong on inspection, not merely unsourced (Section 4.2, and the full research log in `docs/historical_notes.md`).

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Canon Overlap

The presence/absence matrix (`src/align_corpus.align_books`, run on the three canon lists) confirms: 0 books unique to the Protestant list, 0 unique to the Catholic list, and exactly 4 unique to the Orthodox list (1 Esdras, 3 Maccabees, Prayer of Manasseh, Psalm 151) — consistent with the Protestant and Catholic lists both being proper subsets of the Orthodox list under this dataset's baseline Orthodox definition (Section 3.1). The bipartite overlap graph (`notebooks/01_canon_overlap.ipynb`, `outputs/figures/canon_overlap_graph.png`) has 3 tradition nodes, 77 book nodes, and 216 edges. As a spot-check against the case studies motivating this project (Section 1.2): 2 Maccabees resolves to Catholic/Orthodox-only (`{'protestant': False, 'catholic': True, 'orthodox': True}`), and 2 Kings resolves to present in all three — matching the documented canon status of both passages exactly.

### 4.2 Translation Lineage

Two corrections were made against the graph's earlier placeholder edges, documented in full in `docs/historical_notes.md` ("Sourcing the Translation Lineage Graph," 2026-07-19):

1. **The Septuagint does not descend from the Masoretic Text.** The original placeholder graph had a `Hebrew Masoretic Text -> Septuagint` edge. This is anachronistic: the Septuagint was translated c. 250–132 BC [8], while the Masoretes codified and vocalized the Hebrew Masoretic Text roughly a millennium later (7th–10th century AD). Worse, Dead Sea Scroll evidence shows the Septuagint's Hebrew source text (Vorlage) differed in places from the Hebrew text-type that became the Masoretic tradition, rather than being an earlier draft of it [9]. The corrected graph gives both the Septuagint and the Masoretic Text a common ancestor — "Hebrew Source Texts (Second Temple era, textually plural)" — rather than deriving one from the other.

2. **No documented dependency of the King James Version on Luther's Bible.** The placeholder graph asserted a direct `Luther's Bible (1534) -> King James Version (1611)` edge. No such textual dependency is documented; what the placeholder graph omitted entirely is the real, heavily-documented English-Bible lineage. The 1604 rules governing the KJV translators named the Bishops' Bible (1568) as base text, with Tyndale's, Coverdale's, Matthew's, the Great Bible's, and the Geneva Bible's wording to be preferred wherever it fit the original languages better [10]; a computer-assisted sampling study estimates Tyndale's wording at ~84% of the KJV New Testament, and ~76% of the Old Testament portions he completed before his 1536 execution [11]. The corrected graph replaces the Luther edge with `Tyndale's Bible -> Great Bible -> Bishops' Bible -> King James Version`, plus direct edges from Tyndale's Bible and the Geneva Bible to the KJV per the translators' own stated instructions.

A third edge, `Vulgate -> Luther's Bible`, was kept but re-annotated rather than removed: Luther's translation team's primary sources were Hebrew (via team member Caspar Cruciger [12]) and Erasmus's Greek New Testament [13], with the Vulgate serving as a consulted reference (brought by team member Johannes Bugenhagen [12]) rather than a primary source text — a real distinction the earlier version of the graph did not make. The Vulgate's own upstream sourcing was similarly split into its three actual components rather than one generic "Vulgate" predecessor: the Masoretic Hebrew tradition for the protocanonical Old Testament (Jerome's *hebraica veritas* translations, 382–405 AD), the Old Latin (Vetus Latina) for most deuterocanonical books Jerome retained rather than retranslated (Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1–2 Maccabees) [14], and Greek New Testament manuscripts for Jerome's revision of the Old Latin Gospels.

The resulting graph (`notebooks/02_translation_lineage.ipynb`, `outputs/figures/translation_lineage_graph.png`) is a valid directed acyclic graph — verified programmatically (`networkx.is_directed_acyclic_graph`) — of 14 texts and 20 dated, cited edges.

### 4.3 Topic Modeling: Old Testament vs. New Testament

**This section replaces the original protocanonical-vs-deuterocanonical comparison as of the 2026-07-22 scope change.** The original comparison (Catholic corpus, 5,953 passages, plus a genre-confound stress test and topic-count/seed sensitivity sweep) is preserved verbatim in git history at commit `f5b4236` and earlier; it is not reproduced here since it can no longer be regenerated from the current (Protestant-only) corpus, and reporting numbers that can't be rerun against the live pipeline would misrepresent what this thesis currently supports.

Fitting LDA on the 5,262-passage Protestant/KJV corpus produces 12 topics with clearly interpretable top-word lists. Several read as Old-Testament-flavored: Topic 4 ("king, son, said, israel, house, unto, came, lord, david, judah") as royal/dynastic narrative; Topic 7 ("lord, unto, king, israel, moses, people, children, house, aaron, god") as Mosaic/covenant narrative; Topic 8 ("shall, offering, unto, lord, priest, altar, burnt, offerings, holy, offer") as cultic/sacrificial law. Two read as New-Testament-flavored: Topic 5 ("god, ye, unto, things, christ, man, hath, lord, jesus, let") and Topic 6 ("unto, said, came, jesus, went, man, saying, come, saw, ye") both carry direct Christ/Gospel vocabulary ("christ," "jesus").

Comparing topic distribution *within* each category (what fraction of each testament's passages land in each topic, correcting for Old Testament passages outnumbering New Testament roughly 3.4-to-1) shows a clean split at the two Christ-vocabulary topics:

| Topic | Top words | New Testament share | Old Testament share |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | god, ye, unto, things, christ, man, hath, lord, jesus, let | 39.2% | 0.8% |
| 6 | unto, said, came, jesus, went, man, saying, come, saw, ye | 42.0% | 5.1% |

Together, Topics 5 and 6 account for 81.1% of New Testament passages against 6.0% of Old Testament passages — unsurprising given that "jesus" and "christ" are strong, near-exclusively-NT lexical markers, but a useful sanity check that the model is recovering real structure rather than noise. Old Testament passages spread instead across law/cultic topics (Topic 8, 5.6% NT vs. 16.5% OT), Mosaic/covenant narrative (Topic 7, 0.0% NT vs. 10.3% OT — the only topic with zero NT passages), and general prophetic/exhortation topics (Topic 9, "shall, lord, hath, man, earth, come, like, away, men, day," 5.0% NT vs. 21.9% OT; Topic 10, second-person "thou/thy/thee" covenant address, 2.9% NT vs. 14.7% OT).

As a per-book spot check: Psalms' 284 passages concentrate in Topic 10 (55.3%) and Topic 9 (33.5%) — consistent with its dominant second-person covenant/lament register. 2 Kings' 79 passages concentrate in Topic 4 (62.0%) — the royal/dynastic narrative topic, matching Section 1.2's case-study framing of 2 Kings as court-centered dynastic history. John's 126 passages concentrate in Topic 6 (56.3%) and Topic 5 (20.6%) — both Christ-vocabulary topics, as expected for a Gospel. Romans' 63 passages concentrate almost entirely in Topic 5 (90.5%) — consistent with its epistolary, doctrinal-exhortation register differing even from the Gospel narrative topic (Topic 6) that dominates John.

Overall, the observed OT/NT Jensen-Shannon distance (JSD; symmetric, bounded [0, 1], 0 = identical distributions) is **0.765**. A permutation test (3,000 random reshuffles of the OT/NT label across all 5,262 passages, rebuilding the empirical null each time) puts this far outside chance: the null distribution has mean 0.045 and maximum 0.084 across all 3,000 shuffles, giving p = 0.0003 (the floor of 1/3001, i.e. not one shuffle came close to the observed gap).

### 4.4 What This Result Does *Not* Show (and the Section It Replaced)

The 0.765 JSD is real and far from chance, but it answers a much narrower question than the original Section 4.4 (genre-confound test for the protocanonical/deuterocanonical result) did, and the two should not be read as equivalent in strength:

- **No genre control has been run for OT vs. NT.** Old and New Testament differ in genre composition almost completely (Law/Historical Narrative/Wisdom/Prophetic vs. Gospel/Epistle/Apocalyptic) — there is essentially no genre overlap to control for the way the original analysis controlled for genre within the Old Testament alone. A large OT/NT topic gap driven partly or mostly by genre, rather than by "Old Testament content" versus "New Testament content" as such, cannot be ruled out with what has been run so far.
- **No stress test against an obvious confound has been attempted**, unlike the original analysis's genre-confound test (Section 4.5's historical K-sweep) and Prophetic/Baruch small-sample robustness check (bootstrap CI, permutation test) for the deuterocanonical comparison. Both remain open items (Section 5.4).
- **This is an intra-canon result, not a cross-canon one.** It says nothing about canon divergence between Protestant, Catholic, and Orthodox traditions — every passage here is KJV, inside the 66-book Protestant canon. It exists in this thesis because the topic-modeling methodology (Section 3.5) needed *some* comparison to demonstrate on the corpus that remains after the 2026-07-22 scope change, not because it was the question originally posed in Section 1.1.

The original Section 4.4 (genre-confound test) and 4.5 (K-tuning/stability sweep) results below are kept in this draft **as historical record of work already done**, not as current claims about the active corpus — both were run on the now-inactive 73-book Catholic corpus. They are retained because the K-tuning conclusion (`N_TOPICS=12`) is still the value used in Section 4.3 above (Section 3.5 notes this was carried over, not re-tuned), and because deleting completed, sourced work from a thesis draft to make the scope change look cleaner than it is would misrepresent what happened.

**[Historical, corpus no longer active] 4.4a Testing the Genre Confound (protocanonical vs. deuterocanonical, Catholic corpus)**

Section 4.3's original topic-distribution difference (deuterocanonical vs. protocanonical) was consistent with a simpler explanation: the seven deuterocanonical books happen to concentrate in genres (Hellenistic historical narrative for Tobit, Judith, and 1–2 Maccabees; wisdom literature for Wisdom and Sirach) that are comparatively underrepresented among the 39 protocanonical books, so a pure genre effect could produce the result without any distinctively deuterocanonical content difference at all. This was tested by assigning each of the 46 Old Testament books to one of four genre categories — Law, Historical Narrative, Wisdom/Poetic, Prophetic — and comparing protocanonical against deuterocanonical topic distributions *within* each genre that contains both:

| Genre | Protocanonical books | Deuterocanonical books | Passages (proto / deutero) | Within-genre JSD | Baseline JSD | χ² test |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Historical Narrative | 12 (Joshua–Esther) | 4 (Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees) | 1,372 / 374 | **0.636** | 0.567 | χ²=746.1, df=11, p=7×10⁻¹⁵³ |
| Wisdom/Poetic | 5 (Job–Song of Solomon) | 2 (Wisdom, Sirach) | 581 / 277 | **0.296** | 0.567 | χ²=79.8, df=9, p=1.8×10⁻¹³ |
| Prophetic | 17 (Isaiah–Malachi) | 1 (Baruch) | 1,146 / 35 | 0.476 | 0.567 | χ²=84.7, df=10, p=5.9×10⁻¹⁴ |

As a calibration check on scale, the JSD *between* genres among protocanonical passages only (no canon-status difference present, so any distance is a pure genre effect) ranged from 0.469 (Law vs. Historical Narrative) to 0.806 (Historical Narrative vs. Wisdom/Poetic).

Historical Narrative — 4 of the 7 deuterocanonical books, 374 of 686 deuterocanonical passages — showed a within-genre JSD (0.636) *larger* than the unconditioned baseline (0.567), not smaller. Wisdom/Poetic showed a within-genre JSD (0.296) roughly half the baseline. Prophetic, resting on one deuterocanonical book and 35 passages, fell in between (0.476).

**Strengthening the Prophetic comparison.** No second deuterocanonical Prophetic-genre book existed to add. Pearson's chi-square approximation was checked directly and found unreliable for Prophetic (11 of 22 cells, 50%, had expected counts under 5); a bootstrap 95% CI and permutation test (both valid at small n) were used instead:

| Genre | n (deutero) | Observed JSD | Bootstrap 95% CI | Permutation p |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Historical Narrative | 374 | 0.636 | [0.609, 0.677] | 0.0003 |
| Wisdom/Poetic | 277 | 0.296 | [0.249, 0.355] | 0.0003 |
| Prophetic | 35 | 0.476 | [0.390, 0.627] | 0.0003 |

The permutation test was unambiguous for all three genres, including Prophetic — not one of 3,000 reshuffles produced a JSD as large as observed.

### 4.5 Topic-Count Tuning and Stability [Historical — run on the Catholic corpus; `N_TOPICS=12` carried forward to Section 4.3 unchanged, see Section 3.5]

`N_TOPICS=12` (Section 3.5) was a starting point, not a tuned value. Three checks were run: two standard model-selection diagnostics (held-out perplexity and UMass topic coherence [16] across a wide range of candidate topic counts), and — more directly relevant to this thesis's actual claim — whether the Historical Narrative genre-confound result (Section 4.4) holds up under different topic counts and random initializations, or is an artifact of the specific `K=12, random_state=42` used throughout. UMass coherence was implemented directly from the document-term matrix (co-document frequency of each topic's top-10 words) rather than via an external library, for the same avoid-heavy-dependencies reasoning as the LDA-over-BERTopic choice in Section 3.5.

**K sweep**, widened to K ∈ {4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14, 16, 18, 20, 24, 28} (fit on an 80% training split, held-out perplexity and coherence scored there, then the same trained model applied to the full dataset to recheck the Historical Narrative finding):

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | **721.1** (best) | **−1.417** (best) | 0.181 | 0.145 | **No** |
| 6 | 743.4 | −1.464 | 0.335 | 0.349 | Yes |
| 8 | 774.6 | −1.580 | 0.407 | 0.478 | Yes |
| 10 | 797.0 | −1.582 | 0.510 | 0.543 | Yes |
| 12 | 810.2 | −1.655 | 0.551 | 0.610 | Yes |
| 14 | 855.5 | −1.710 | 0.474 | 0.479 | Yes |
| 16 | 876.4 | −1.752 | 0.476 | 0.454 | No (narrowly) |
| 18 | 910.6 | −1.835 | 0.520 | 0.524 | Yes |
| 20 | 944.3 | −1.879 | 0.538 | 0.607 | Yes |
| 24 | 998.0 | −1.882 | 0.509 | 0.545 | Yes |
| 28 | 1070.8 | −1.908 | 0.519 | 0.545 | Yes |

Both held-out perplexity and UMass coherence increase/worsen monotonically across the entire tested range — by either metric alone, K=4 is the "best" fit, and fit quality degrades steadily as K grows all the way to 28. But K=4 is exactly where the Historical Narrative finding fails, and fails worst: at four topics the model is too coarse to represent the thematic distinction this analysis depends on, and both JSD values collapse to a fraction of their size at every other K tested. The finding holds at 9 of the 11 K values tested — everything from K=6 through K=28 except K=16, whose shortfall is narrow (0.454 vs. 0.476) rather than a reversal.

**Seed sweep** (K=12, full data, three different `random_state` values):

| `random_state` | Baseline JSD | Historical Narrative JSD | Historical Narrative ≥ baseline? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 0.502 | 0.636 | Yes |
| 42 | 0.567 | 0.636 | Yes |
| 99 | 0.506 | 0.546 | Yes |

At the topic count used throughout this thesis, the finding holds across every seed tested.

**Decision: keep `N_TOPICS=12`.** It is not perplexity- or coherence-optimal in the tested range — nothing tested is, except the substantively-failing K=4 — but it sits well inside the broad K=6–28 range (one isolated exception, K=16) where the genre-confound finding consistently replicates, it is where this notebook's topic-word interpretations (Section 4.3) were actually done, and it is robust to random seed. Optimizing K purely against perplexity or coherence here would mean discarding the one property those metrics don't measure and this analysis needs most: enough topic resolution to detect the effect being tested for. This is a known, general tension in topic-model selection — statistical fit metrics reward whatever compresses word co-occurrence patterns best, which is not the same target as "enough resolution for a specific downstream comparison" — not a problem specific to this corpus.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 What the Topic Result Does and Doesn't Show

**Revised 2026-07-22.** The current active result (Section 4.3) is that Old and New Testament passages, within the single-translation Protestant/KJV corpus, occupy measurably different regions of topic space (JSD 0.765, permutation p = 0.0003), driven overwhelmingly by two topics that carry direct Christ/Gospel vocabulary and are almost entirely New Testament. This is expected — Jesus is a New Testament figure by definition — but the value of running it at all is methodological: it confirms the LDA/passage-chunking pipeline recovers real, interpretable structure (Section 3.5) on the corpus that survives the scope change, which the per-book spot checks (Psalms, 2 Kings, John, Romans in Section 4.3) reinforce independently.

What this result does **not** show, stated plainly rather than left implicit: it is not a genre-controlled result the way the original protocanonical/deuterocanonical finding was (Section 4.4's historical record). Old and New Testament differ in genre, era, and register all at once, with essentially no genre overlap between them to control for — so "OT and NT passages are topically distinguishable" is close to the least surprising quantitative claim this pipeline could produce, and should not be read as evidence of anything beyond "the topic model works." The genre-confound methodology from the original analysis (Section 4.4a) does not transfer to this comparison, because it depended on there being genres shared between the two groups being compared (Historical Narrative and Wisdom/Poetic both had protocanonical and deuterocanonical representatives); OT and NT do not share genre categories in the same way. A more informative version of this analysis would need either a different confound to test against, or a return to the original cross-canon comparison — see Section 6.

The original genre-confound finding (Section 4.4a, historical record) remains true of the Catholic corpus as run: for Historical Narrative, which carried most of the deuterocanonical corpus, genre did not explain the topic-distribution effect away — the within-genre gap was larger than the unconditioned baseline. For Wisdom/Poetic, genre explained roughly half the effect. That finding is no longer reproducible from the active pipeline (Section 4.3's note) but is not retracted — it was a real result on the corpus that existed when it was computed, and is preserved rather than deleted.

### 5.2 The Translation Lineage Corrections, Read Together

The two corrected edges in Section 4.2 share a pattern worth naming directly: both were plausible-sounding claims that happened to be wrong once checked against sources, not merely unsourced guesses that turned out to be roughly right. The Septuagint-from-Masoretic-Text edge is wrong because it assumes "the Hebrew Bible" was a single fixed text that different traditions each took a copy of at different points in history, when in fact multiple Hebrew textual traditions coexisted well into the Second Temple period, and which one a given translator worked from was a real, consequential choice — the same choice Jerome explicitly defended in his *hebraica veritas* argument against contemporaries who trusted the Septuagint instead. The Luther-to-KJV edge is wrong because it substitutes the most *famous* prior Reformation translation for the actual documented one (the English Tyndale/Bishops'-Bible tradition), which is a natural error to make without checking primary sources. Both errors are the kind that intuition produces and citation-checking catches — which is the methodological point of doing the sourcing work at all (Section 3.6), not a criticism specific to this project's earlier draft.

### 5.3 Descriptive Framing, Revisited

Section 1.3 commits this project to descriptive rather than evaluative claims. The results in Section 4 hold to that: nothing here argues that the Protestant, Catholic, or Orthodox canon is theologically correct, or (in the historical Section 4.4a record) that deuterocanonical books are more or less inspired, authoritative, or valuable than protocanonical ones. The current topic-modeling result (Section 4.3) is more modest than the original in what it can show — it demonstrates the pipeline recovers real structure, not a canon-divergence claim (Section 5.1) — but the same descriptive-not-evaluative commitment applies to it. The translation lineage graph is similarly descriptive throughout: it shows what fed into what, not which resulting text is more faithful to an original.

### 5.4 Limitations and Open Items

Being explicit about what is not yet done, rather than presenting a more finished picture than the pipeline actually supports:

- **The current topic-modeling result (Section 4.3) has not been genre-controlled**, unlike the original protocanonical/deuterocanonical comparison (Section 4.4a). Since OT and NT share almost no genre categories, the original within-genre methodology does not directly transfer; a different confound-testing approach would need to be designed for this comparison specifically. Until that is done, Section 4.3's result should be read narrowly (Section 5.1), not as evidence of a specific *kind* of OT/NT difference.
- **`N_TOPICS=12` was carried over from the K-tuning sweep run on the now-inactive 73-book Catholic corpus (Section 4.5), not re-tuned for the 66-book Protestant/KJV corpus.** Whether K=12 is still a reasonable choice for this different corpus (different size, different genre mix) is untested.
- **The topic count (12), on the corpus it was tuned against, was not perplexity- or coherence-optimal.** Section 4.5's historical widened sweep (K ∈ {4, 6, ..., 28}, Catholic corpus) showed both metrics monotonically favoring K=4, the smallest value tested; K=12 was kept there because K=4 was exactly where the (then-active) genre-confound finding failed. This tension is a known, general property of topic-model selection, not specific to this corpus.
- **The K sweep and UMass coherence self-implementation have not been cross-checked against an external library (e.g. gensim's `CoherenceModel`)**, and both were run only on the now-inactive Catholic corpus.
- **LDA vs. embedding-based topic modeling.** Bag-of-words LDA was chosen for practical reasons (Section 3.5), not because it is expected to be the best-performing method. Never run: an embedding-based approach (e.g. BERTopic) on either the current or the original corpus.
- **The cross-canon topic-modeling comparison (protocanonical vs. deuterocanonical, Section 4.4a) is no longer part of the active pipeline**, following the 2026-07-22 decision to restrict the text source to the Protestant Bible. This is the largest single open item: it removes the topic-modeling component's connection to the thesis's stated research question (canon *divergence*) almost entirely, leaving Section 4.3 as a narrower, mostly-methodological placeholder. Reinstating the original comparison (recoverable from git history) is one option; designing a new cross-canon-compatible question that survives the Protestant-only constraint is another (Section 6).
- **The Orthodox canon list (used only in Sections 4.1–4.2, which are unaffected by the text-source scope change) is one baseline, not a jurisdictional standard.** Section 3.1 notes this; the 77-book list does not represent Slavonic, Georgian, or Ethiopian Orthodox canons, which include additional books not modeled here.
- **Related Work (Section 2) needs specific citations**, not category descriptions, before this is submission-ready.

## 6. Conclusion and Future Work

This project set out to test whether standard computational text-analysis methods — set comparison, graph construction, topic modeling — could make canon divergence across the Protestant, Catholic, and Orthodox biblical traditions visible as data, using the same tools applicable to any other corpus-comparison problem, without adjudicating which tradition's canon is theologically correct. Two of the three components still do exactly that. The canon overlap analysis (Section 4.1) reproduces the documented canon differences exactly, including the specific case-study pair (2 Maccabees, 2 Kings) that motivated the project. The translation lineage graph (Section 4.2) demonstrates that even a small, deliberately scoped historical-lineage claim benefits materially from source-checking — two of eight original edges were substantively wrong, not merely unsourced. Neither draws on comparative Bible text, so neither was affected by the 2026-07-22 decision to restrict the project's text source to the Protestant Bible (KJV) only.

The third component — topic modeling — was affected directly, and this thesis states that plainly rather than reframing the smaller remaining result as equivalent to the original. The original comparison (protocanonical vs. deuterocanonical content, genre-confound-tested, K-and-seed-stability-tested) found a real, interpretable thematic difference that survived its most obvious alternative explanation for the genre carrying most of the deuterocanonical corpus; that result is preserved in git history and in Section 4.4a as a historical record, not deleted, but is no longer reproducible from the current pipeline and is not claimed as an active finding of this draft. In its place, Section 4.3 reports a much narrower Old-vs-New-Testament comparison on the surviving corpus — real (JSD 0.765, p = 0.0003) but explicitly not genre-controlled and not connected to canon divergence, the thesis's actual research question.

Immediate next steps, in rough priority order, reordered to reflect the scope change: (1) decide, as a research-direction question rather than a purely technical one, whether to reinstate the cross-canon topic comparison (the Douay-Rheims and Septuagint fetchers and raw data remain in the repository specifically so this is not costly to undo) or to develop a new comparison that both respects the Protestant-only text constraint and still speaks to the thesis's stated research question — Section 4.3 as it stands does neither well; (2) if the Protestant-only scope is kept, design and run a genre-control analogue for the OT/NT result, since none currently exists (Section 5.4); (3) resources permitting, run a BERTopic comparison and cross-check the self-implemented UMass coherence against an external library, on whichever corpus is active once (1) is decided.
